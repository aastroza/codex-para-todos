# Blog editorial guide

This blog should make Codex and agentic work understandable to a broad Spanish-speaking audience. Write for curious readers first, not only for people already close to software, startups, or university programs.

## What changed in the current article draft

- The article moved from an internal course recap toward a public-facing reflection.
- The opening now starts with a concrete scene from the final exam instead of background about the course.
- The piece keeps the author's first-person judgment, including what was surprising, difficult, and worth showing.
- The Showcase ExploraTech is framed as a curated selection, not a ranking of the best projects.
- The links point readers to the general Showcase ExploraTech page instead of scattering attention across individual project links.
- Screenshots and captions are part of the story. They should help the reader see what students built, not decorate the article.
- The central idea is professional development: students need to learn how to work in an environment where agents can operate software.
- The recurring phrase "velocidad máquina, pero con criterio humano" is part of the blog's voice for this topic.

## Editorial direction

- Prefer personal essays grounded in concrete examples.
- Keep the audience broad. Explain technical ideas through what people do, what changes, and what choices they face.
- Start with a scene, tension, question, or specific observation. Avoid institutional throat-clearing.
- Use first person when the article comes from lived teaching, building, or community work.
- Show the work. Mention constraints, failed assumptions, and the practical decisions behind a result.
- Avoid hype. Do not describe AI as magic or destiny. Show what happened.
- Avoid formulaic contrasts such as "no es X, es Y" unless the contrast is genuinely the point.
- Avoid generic AI prose. Prefer plain verbs, direct nouns, and details from the actual work.
- Use links sparingly. Choose the one next step that matters for the reader.
- Use images when they carry evidence. Add captions that interpret the image in plain language.
- Preserve names, dates, institutions, and course names exactly when they are part of the record.

## Blog post checklist

- The title gives a general reader a reason to click.
- The first two paragraphs contain a real scene or concrete claim.
- The article can be understood by someone who does not program.
- Every technical term earns a short explanation through use.
- The piece has one main idea and one final action.
- Screenshots have alt text and captions.
- Claims about students, projects, tools, and dates are sourced from the repo or the author.
- Before publishing, run `npm run build` and the `write-with-zinsser` AI-tell scanner when editing long prose.
